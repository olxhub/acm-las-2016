In recent years, Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs) have offered an unprecedented opportunity for individuals to access university level education [1, 2].

Many universities have joined the bandwagon with different agendas – from the need to become more visible and competitive to more aspirational goals such as social engagement and universal access to education for all [1, 3].

By opening the gates to a more diverse audience, there has been a renewed interest in understanding how to provide educational opportunities that go beyond what is offered in traditional brick-and-mortar courses. While there has been an exponential growth in exploring learning, teaching and effectiveness of MOOCs – there appears to be a lack of research documenting and understanding the value of the student experience [4].

Of course there are also those who take a more critical stance and the Times Magazine cover below provides a worrying picture: 1 teacher to 160.000 students ratio (is this where education is going?)
