“Currently we know little about MOOC learners, about how to design and deliver successfully in a range of subjects, and most importantly at a range of levels (eg final year undergrad). Is the experience helpful to learners, and do they get value from their certificates of completion? Much more research is needed, and perhaps JISC might find this a useful area in which to support the UK HE community”

Jeff Haywood (See full post)

The quote above highlights two important problems: on one hand how little we know about the learners or partitipants in MOOCs, on the other the limited understanding of how to design engaging and useful learning experiences in this envionment.

Although both are intrinsically connected to the ideas of student experience, one thing that is common are the reasons for enrolling in MOOCs.

The motivations, goals and intents which individual partitipating in MOOCs implicitly or explicitly carry when they engage with MOOCs is probably one of the most problematic aspect in our ability to understand learners' needs and to tailor experiences which are useful to for them.

 

The following selection provides an overview of what commentators pushed in the media about what we know about learners, why they 'drop out' and why the existing pedagogical model is not adequate.
Far from being comprehensive, the list of sources is provided to give an idea of the complexity of the issues involved.

More recently, Zhengao et al. [5] provide the Coursera view about who the people enrolling are and how they benefit from the MOOCs. The figure below points to both tangible and intagible benefits, which somewhat are suggestive about the reasons why people enroll.

Yet, the picture is more complex. Koller et al., talking about Coursera participants said:

“Some students enroll on a whim, to see what a course is about, to figure out whether a particular topic might be worth pursuing, or out of curiosity regarding online education in general. Other students sign up for a handful of classes with the idea of shopping around to find a good fit. Yet other students enroll in a MOOC in much the same way that one might "bookmark" an interesting web page for future reference. ”

Koller, Ng, Do and Chen (2013)

Such complexity is also demonstrated in the Workshop on goal setting presented at LAK 16.
In one of the papers, McIntyre et al. showed that when asking the question explicitly, there are a range of reasons why participants engage, but these are not necessarily consistent with what they do in practice.
