UNSW Australia entered the MOOC space with the specific intention to explore pedagogical models working at scale in online courses by maximizing the combination of different pedagogical design features and modes of delivery.

To demonstrate the way in which we designed evaluations fitting the various settings, we selected MOOCs with specific characteriestics.The four MOOCs differ in multiple ways: disciplines, target groups and pedagogical models – despite the fact they share the same building blocks.

The first section of Table 1 (top) presents details about each course: duration, target group, course design and delivery method. Course design refers to the way the course modules were released. In ‘all-at once’, all modules were released to learners at the start of the course. In ‘sequential’ course modules were released to learners week by week in a sequential order. In ‘semi-sequential’ course modules were grouped and released sequentially. Course delivery refers to how the teaching was structured throughout the MOOC. ‘Staggered’ refers course instructors providing regular instruction to guide learning. ‘All-at-once’ refers to instructors not providing scaffolds to direct learners into modules – leaving the option to learners themselves. The second section distills the size of the courses into videos (main content), quizzes (formative activities), assignment (summative activities) and forums (social interactions). The third section details the different roles of the discussion forums across the four MOOCs, their structure/depth, and their usage. The final section provides the funnel of participation [11], with percentages of those who appeared during the length of the course (‘active learners’), and those who completed the course (defined as a pass or higher).
